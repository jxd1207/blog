What’s the Analog layout?

Posted on April 22, 2023 by Xiangdong Jia

Analog layout is the process of designing the physical layout of analog integrated circuits, involving placing and routing transistors, capacitors, and other circuit components to create a functional circuit that meets the desired performance specifications. Analog circuits are used in a wide range of applications, including communication systems, power management, and sensors.

It's a common misconception that analog layout is a piece of cake in the semiconductor industry. While I won't deny that completing a DRC/LVS layout is a straightforward task, it's only a small part of what an analog layout engineer does. The real challenge lies in meeting the designers' requirements and making the circuit competitive in the market. After all, PPA (power, performance, area) is on everyone's minds nowadays, and achieving those targets requires a whole team effort.

Analog designers and analog layout engineers are like the Yin and Yang in the design circle. The designer is the Yin, representing the creative and intuitive side of the process, while the layout engineer is the Yang, representing the logical and practical side. In China, a famous philosophy called Taoism emphasizes the balance between Yin and Yang. Just as Yin and Yang must be balanced to achieve harmony, analog designers and layout engineers must work together to create a successful circuit.

However, just like in Taoism, there can be some disagreement between the Yin and Yang. The designer might say, "I want this component to be placed here!" and the layout engineer might reply, "But that will cause DRC violations!" But with communication and compromise, they can find a way to achieve balance and harmony in the circuit.

As mentioned before, the analog layout is an essential step in the semiconductor design process because the layout of a circuit can significantly impact its performance. For instance, the placement of components can affect the parasitic capacitance and resistance of the circuit, which can cause unwanted noise. The routings can also affect the circuit's speed and power consumption. So, that’s why layout engineers are on the logical land practical side.

To create a successful analog layout, designers need to have a strong understanding of circuit theory, semiconductor physics, and fabrication processes. They must also balance competing requirements, such as performance, power consumption, and area constraints.
